Albert Einstein spent his early years in the city of Ulm, Germany, where his family ran a small electrochemical business. Despite facing challenges in his education, Einstein's curiosity and intellect flourished, laying the foundation for his future contributions to theoretical physics.

Einstein's academic journey led him to the renowned Swiss Federal Institute of Technology (ETH Zurich) and later to the University of Zurich, where he earned his doctorate in 1905. This pivotal year, known as his annus mirabilis, saw the publication of groundbreaking papers that laid the foundation for modern physics.

Albert Einstein's most famous contributions came in the form of his theories of relativity. The special theory of relativity, published in 1905, and the general theory of relativity, presented in 1915, revolutionized our understanding of space, time, and gravity, solidifying Einstein's status as one of the greatest minds in scientific history.

Albert Einstein passed away on April 18, 1955, but his legacy lives on through his enduring impact on science and humanity. His contributions to theoretical physics continue to shape our understanding of the cosmos, and his famous equation, E=mc^2, remains a symbol of the interconnectedness of energy, mass, and the profound mysteries of the universe.
